Typography is the balance and interplay of letterforms on the page—a verbal and visual equation that helps the reader understands the form and absorbs the substance of the page content. Typography plays a dual role as both verbal and visual communication. When users scan a page they are subconsciously aware of both functions: first they survey the overall graphic patterns of the page, and then they parse the language and read.

Good typography establishes a visual hierarchy for rendering prose on the page by providing visual punctuation and graphic accents that help readers understand relations between prose and pictures, headlines and subordinate blocks of text.

1. Fonts

U-LEAF uses bold typography to create stronger hierarchies and drama across the site. We’re moving from Verdana to Arial as the U-LEAF’s default web font for headers, elements, form labels, sub headers and body copy.

2. Type Sizes

Large bold type should be used to establish a clear information hierarchy. These are the recommended type sizes.

3. Putting it together - Hierarchy

Varying type size is one of the best ways to differentiate content. Different sizes of type, used consistently throughout your pages, will signal loud and clear to U-LEAF users the relative importance of pages' elements. We can also use different styles; for example, all-caps, or using italic for sub-headings. Serif and sans serif faces can also be mixed to good effect.

4. Spacing

It is recommended to leave blank spaces in pages. This negative or white space will help focus attention on the text—and it's the text that speaks loudest, so let it be heard. Remember the line-height CSS property; a good rule of thumb is line-spacing that's at least 140% of text size. Designers spend countless hours attempting to achieve a balance between the black of the type and negative or white space that it envelops. Likewise, we should take time to consider the macro white space, the 'voids' that shape our blocks of text.

Iconography

The purpose of this guidelines is to help you to create new or use pre-designed icons aiming to achieve consistent visual language for U-LEAF .

This document comprises the recommendations on their creation and use. Our new icons should be pure, distinct and friendly. They are designed to make it faster to find what you are looking for.

The following principles have been applied when designing the U-LEAF icons.

  • Universal: To ensure success, icons need to be universally understood by a global audience. It should enable them to quickly recognize U-LEAF.
  • Affordance: The user should immediately recognize what the graphic means and how to use it.
  • Originality: Most of brand identity is being unique to the degree to be recognizable.
  • Aesthetic: Sure it's low rez and often monochrome but it has to be nice to look at.
  • Simplicity: Icons need to communicate the essence of U-LEAF application– so simplicity is key. Focus on the most distinguishable feature of U-LEAF and forget the rest. The icons rely on the purest visual elements to convey ideas in the most immediate way.

1. Categories

Each icon set has a specific purpose within U-LEAF, so in total we have six different categories covering all visual aspects of icon usage across U-LEAF Application. Icons can be presented at three sizes (16, 24 and 32 pixels).

This provides complete flexibility when applying icons to page designs.

    • 3. Using Icons

    When designing and exporting for U-LEAF application, it's essential to create high quality application icons that scale well in the various places the icon appears. Below are a number of rules that one must adhere to get the best from each icon and its intended communication:

    • Icons should be drawn using vector-based software such as Adobe Illustrator.
    • Draw icons to the intended finish size (16, 24 or 32 pixels squared). Do not draw icons at arbitrary sizes and then scale them to the intended sizes. Doing so will result in sub-pixel values, and blurry lines.
    • All horizontal and vertical strokes must be drawn at whole pixel values (eg 4 pixels wide, not 4.5 pixels wide). This will prevent blurry edges.
    • Angles should be restricted to multiples of 15 degrees (15, 30, 45, etc). This is especially important for the smaller icons.
